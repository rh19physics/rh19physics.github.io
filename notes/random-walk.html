Random Walk Visualization

Visualization · Mathematics & Physics · Originally written by me on Mar 12, 2026

My interest in random walks, stochastic processes, and Markov chains grew out of my research on ion stochastic heating, which is one of the key mechanisms behind turbulence dissipation in weakly collisional plasmas.

In space plasma physics, stochastic heating is typically described something like this: large-amplitude turbulent electromagnetic fluctuations break the gyromotion of ions, violating the first adiabatic invariant (the magnetic moment) and producing stochastic orbits. As a result, ions gain energy in a manner akin to a random walk, with heating occurring predominantly perpendicular to the magnetic field.

Phrases like "a manner akin to a random walk" kept appearing, and I realized I didn't have a strong intuition for what it really meant. So I did some search and I came across the famous quote by Shizuo Kakutani: "A drunk man will find his way home, but a drunk bird may get lost forever." In other words, a random walk in 1D or 2D is guaranteed to return to the origin, but in 3D and higher dimensions, there's a nonzero probability of never returning.

I touched on the intuition behind this in my talk at the 2026 PSP Turbulence and Dissipation Working Group: whether a random walk returns to the origin comes down to a competition between how fast the walker spreads diffusively and how fast the available space grows with dimension.

To build some visual intuition, I animated comparisons of 1D, 2D, and 3D random walks using Manim. The code is available on my GitHub repo. It's still rough, but I may refine it later.

4   Theorems & proofs

Theorem 1 (Parseval's theorem).

If \(f(x)\) is square-integrable on \([-L, L]\) with Fourier coefficients \(c_n\), then \[ \frac{1}{2L}\int_{-L}^{L} |f(x)|^2\,\dd x = \sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} |c_n|^2. \]

Proof.

Expand \(|f|^2 = f\bar{f}\) in Fourier series and integrate term by term, using orthogonality of \(e^{i n\pi x/L}\).

import numpy as np

def energy_spectrum(B, dx):
    """Compute the magnetic energy spectrum."""
    Bk = np.fft.rfft(B) * dx
    k  = np.fft.rfftfreq(len(B), d=dx) * 2 * np.pi
    Ek = 0.5 * np.abs(Bk)**2 / len(B)
    return k, Ek
